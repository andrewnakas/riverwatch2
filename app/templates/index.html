How it works

RiverWatch2 predicts how much water (in cfs — cubic feet per second) will flow past each gauge for the next 14 days. It does this by combining several independent forecasters into one ensemble, then trusting each one in proportion to how accurate it has recently been at that gauge.

The forecasters (members):

  • Persistence — "tomorrow looks like today." A simple, hard-to-beat baseline.
  • Runoff model — learns how rain, snowmelt, and soil moisture turn into streamflow at this basin.
  • AI time-series models (Chronos, TTM, TimesFM) — large pretrained models that recognize flow patterns without per-river training.
  • National Water Model — NOAA's physics-based national river simulation.

How the blend is built:

  1. Each member is backtested against this gauge's own history, across several past windows spanning different seasons and flow levels (not just the last few months), so its accuracy is measured fairly.
  2. Members get a weight proportional to 1 / error² — the more accurate one recently was, the bigger its say. If one is clearly best, the blend leans almost entirely on it.
  3. Each forecast is gently anchored to the last observed flow so the curve starts where the river actually is, then relaxes toward the model's own prediction over a few days.
  4. The shaded band around the line is a 90% range: we expect the real flow to land inside it about 9 days out of 10.

When to be cautious: if a gauge stops reporting, the forecast is flagged stale (it's projecting from old data). A degraded flag means most models were unavailable and the system fell back to the simple baseline. These appear in the per-station notes so you always know how much to trust a given forecast.

Covers free-flowing whitewater rivers (no dam-release runs). Forecasts refresh every few hours. Data: USGS gauges, NOAA weather & snow, NOAA National Water Model.
